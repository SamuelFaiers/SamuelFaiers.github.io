Usebi

Usebi is a mobile app that helps home cooks keep track of the food in their kitchen and reduce their food waste.

Background

As of January 2020, UK households generate a total of 4.5m tonnes of food waste each year. This amounts to £14bn worth of food and a severe environmental impact. How can I help people reduce the amount the food waste they produce? I started this passion project to try and solve the problem.

Solution

Usebi lets home cooks create an inventory of all their food and leftovers, including expiry dates. Push notifications remind them of food that is close to expiring, allowing them to eat or use it before it goes off and has to be thrown away.

Outcome

Participants in the final usability test successfully completed 90% of the tasks with little or no difficulty. Usebi as a concept and product was also well received by all but one of the participants in both usability tests, indicating a strong product market fit.

Product overview

Food inventory

The inventory forms the backbone of the product. Here users can add all of their food and leftovers to keep track of them. Both are placed in separate categories, accessible via tabs in the top navigation bar. This makes it easier to navigate the inventory and makes its contents easier to digest (no pun intended). Users can also search and filter their inventory to check for individual or groups of items.

Adding food to the inventory

When adding food to the inventory, the key information users need to provide is the expiry date. This allows notifications to be sent to them as that date approaches.

The type of expiry date determines what is advised if the food expires. With “use by” dates, expired food should be thrown away because it could be dangerous to eat. For “best before” dates, the food is still safe to eat, but should be eaten as soon as possible before its quality deteriorates any further.

Adding leftovers to the inventory

When adding leftovers there is an option to include the main ingredients. This, along with the name, helps the product suggest where they should be stored.

Because leftovers do not have set expiry dates, when they expire is determined by their storage. According to the NHS, refrigerated leftovers should be eaten within 2 days. Frozen leftovers last indefinitely, but should be eaten as soon as possible after 3 months because they will begin to deteriorate in quality.

Reminder notifications

Notifications remind the user of their food and leftovers, increasing in frequency as the expiry dates approach. Those that need to be used ASAP or thrown away appear at the top of the inventory, regardless of what filters are in place. This ensures anything that needs to be used or thrown away is always clearly visible. Push notifications also make the user aware of their food and leftovers outside of the product.

Favouriting a food item

If the user frequently adds the same food and leftovers to their inventory, they have the option to save them to their favourites. They can then easily re-add them to the inventory in the future, without having to go through the usual process.

Research

Although I had a solution in mind based on my own experiences, the product needed to meet the needs of home cooks as a wider group, not just my own. Therefore, I conducted generative research to uncover the main experiences and frustrations home cooks have when managing food waste. The findings showed that my initial idea only addressed solved one of the frustrations that home cooks have.

Survey

I sent out a survey to quickly gather quantitative data and find out if food waste is an issue for home cooks specifically. If not, I would need to rethink the target user group. The results showed that a large proportion of home cooks do struggle with food waste and gave some insight into why. From 103 vetted respondents, I found that:

  • 49.5% of home cooks throw away leftovers more than once a month
  • 47.6% of home cooks throw away unused ingredients more than once a month
  • 95% cite signs of spoilage as a main reason for throwing away food
  • 58.3% cite expiry dates as a main reason for throwing away food

User interviews

Having gained some insight into home cook’s experiences with food waste from the survey, I conducted user interviews to delve deeper into their thoughts and feelings around the issue. This would help me identify their key frustrations, as well as their root causes, allowing me to find solutions that solve the underlying problem.

  • The main priority for home cooks when planning and preparing their meals is to use up the food they have before it expires
  • When their food does expire, it is most often because they forgot they had it stored in their kitchen
  • Almost all participants (4/5) said they often have leftover food after cooking a meal

Persona

Based on the results of my research, I created a persona to help me empathise with the user. My aim was for the persona’s goals and frustrations to inform every design decision I make and help me prioritise what features to include first. I named him “Fred the Forgetful Foodie” to make him easy to remember.

Design goals

The persona helped me establish three goals for the project. All of these goals were chosen to align the product with Fred’s main goals and solve his frustrations.

  • Help home cooks keep track of the food in their kitchen
  • Help home cooks remember food that is close to expiring
  • Help home cooks use their leftovers and ingredients before they expire

Ideation

Based on the design goals above, I began exploring ideas for the product. I then narrowed them down to one key concept. This concept will allow Fred to create an inventory of the food and leftovers in his kitchen and help him keep track of them. Push notifications will remind him of any that are close to expiring, allowing him to use them before they go off and have to be thrown away.

Focusing on this one concept ensured that the first iteration of the project could be completed within the timeframe I had set. I chose this one over the others because it addressed all three design goals, while still being one of the most feasible. The other ideas could then be added with future iterations if they make the cut.

User flow

Having decided on the main concept, I designed key user flows through the lens of the persona. This helped me determine how to structure the app and the steps that needed to be included in the prototype and tested.

Sketches

With a better idea of how the app should be structured, I began sketching ideas for important screens. Paper was the ideal medium for this, as it allowed me to very quickly explore different layouts without worrying too much about the details.

Low fidelity prototype

I then created a low fidelity prototype using Figma. This was so that I could test the functionality of the product, without spending too much time designing layouts and flows that may end up performing poorly in usability tests.

Usability test

I conducted a usability test on the prototype in order to validate my solutions and identify any pain points. It was remotely tested on 5 participants who were given 6 tasks to complete.

Iterating the prototype

The results from the usability test identified two key pain points that needed to be addressed.

Problem

When adding leftovers, it was difficult to include the main ingredients by filling in the field and tapping the “+” button.

Solution

Main ingredients can now also be added by filling in the field and tapping “Go” or “Done” on their OS keyboard.

Problem

Favourites were difficult to find, due to them being within the user's profile.

Solution

Favourites are now accessible via the main bottom navigation bar.

User interface

Design system

To ensure visual consistency throughout the product, I created a design system. This established key principles around layout, typography, colour and iconography. The asset library contained components repeated throughout the app, making them easy to find and make global changes to if necessary.

Visual design

The aim of the visual design was for it to loosely resemble a modern home kitchen, making the user feel at home when using the product. For example, all corners are slightly rounded to resemble the bevelled corners of kitchen counters. The primary colour draws the user’s attention to key elements and was inspired by the more vibrant kitchens that utilise shades of teal and green. To ensure the product was usable by anyone, I prioritised accessibility over aesthetics where necessary for elements like text sizing, colour contrast and target sizes.

Illustrations

Each illustration represents a category of dishes or ingredients that the user can select when adding food or leftovers to their inventory. I designed the illustration style to use simple shapes, consisting only of shades of green, orange and white. This was to make them consistent with the rest of the UI, but also make it quick and easy to reproduce their style whenever new ones are required.

Lessons learned & next steps

Working on this project helped me learn more about conducting user research. Doing so allowed me to create a user centred experience that helps solve a large scale problem. It also reaffirmed that designing solutions solely based on assumptions should be avoided, as my initial idea before conducting research would have only solved a small part of the problem.

It is worth mentioning that I used a platform called Userbrain to conduct the usability tests. I chose this method because it allowed me to validate the prototypes quickly at a relatively low cost in the middle of a global pandemic. In the future I would like to use more conventional methods to gather more reliable data.

The next step I would like to take is to include an onboarding flow that introduces the product to new users. This will most likely guide them through adding their first food item to the inventory. Following that I plan to explore the following:

Inventory improvements: At the moment there are two categories. However, the “Food” category could (and probably should) be split into several more to make it easier to sort and navigate. “Ingredients,” “Beverages” and “Read meals” are examples of possible candidates.

Scanning: the ability to scan food items into the inventory would automate the process and make it much easier for the user. First, I will need to look into what sort of information stored within bar codes to see if this idea is feasible.

Recipes: another idea I have is similar a traditional recipe app. It would allow users to upload and share recipes that use up leftovers as well as ingredients. These recipes could even be filtered based on the food and leftovers in each user’s inventory.
