Disciple: Website UX

Disciple is a SaaS product that lets community leaders build their own independent community platform on web, iOS and Android.

Background

Disciple’s marketing website had previously been built using solely quantitative data obtained from analytics and A/B tests. These methods gave a good idea of the success of every iteration (in terms of numbers) but did not give any insight into the thoughts and feelings of visitors or their perception of Disciple.

Solution

With approval from the key stakeholders, I took it upon myself to conduct user research, taking a more human centred approach to improving the website. Based on the results, I made recommendations for improvements that would enhance the visitor’s perception and understanding of Disciple.

Outcome

My recommendations improved the success rate of usability tests to 100%. As a result, they are currently being implemented onto the website. So far, the first improvement has increased the conversion rate by 24%. The results also led to user research being embedded into the design process, a huge win for Disciple, its customers, and their end-users.

Campaigning for UX

The first challenge was to persuade Disciple’s upper management to approve the conduction of user research. Their concern was the time and money required to conduct the research without the guarantee of success. Ultimately, a compromise was made, which would be an opportunity to prove the value of conducting user research in the design and development process.

It was decided that I should focus my efforts on the homepage as a proof of concept. This page was chosen because existing data showed that very few visitors accessed went to the other pages before either converting or leaving the website. Therefore, the homepage was deemed the most important page to improve for this project.

Usability test

I started by conducting two usability tests. This was to gain insight into the thoughts and feelings of visitors while interacting with the website and to identify any issues that inhibited them from completing key tasks. Both tests were conducted remotely, using “Userbrain” to recruit five participants each. The tasks they were given were also identical.

The difference was that the first test gave participants access to the entire website, while in the second test only the homepage was accessible. The intention behind the first test was to identify any content within other pages that effectively helped participants complete the tasks. This content could then be used to inform that on the homepage. At the same time, I wanted to see how successful the homepage alone was in helping the participants complete their tasks, allowing me to determine which areas could be improved.

Key insights

"What is it though?"

Very few participants were able to accurately identify Disciple’s purpose. The three who did were of those who had access to the entire website and thoroughly explored all its content.

“This looks a bit outdated”

The top fold of the homepage left a poor first impression. Participants were unimpressed with the design which they felt looked outdated.

“There’s a lot to scroll through”

The participants found the amount of content on the homepage overwhelming, which led to some being frustrated with the amount of scrolling they had to do.

““How can we improve the homepage to convey information more concisely, making it easier to understand the purpose of Disciple and see it as a valuable product?””

My recommendations

Bring key visuals out of hiding

My first recommendation was to bring key visual content out of the explainer video in the top fold of the homepage, replacing it with a more concise product visual. Another designer took this further, suggesting we use a real example of a community platform that had been built with Disciple.

Using a genuine example of a community platform in action would help visitors visualise the product and therefore understand its purpose. It would also act as social proof, persuading them that Disciple is a valuable product that has made its customers successful.

Disciple's makeover

Disciple was also undergoing a large scale rebrand at the same time as this project. Thanks to the branding team, the visual quality website was greatly improved. This would improve first impressions by representing Disciple as an established and valuable product.

Update product visuals

The product visuals on the homepage consisted of out of date low-quality GIFs. Higher quality and up to date visuals would be more engaging and more informative to visitors. I therefore created a new set of animated visuals that gave a quick tour of key product features. These were exported as videos rather than GIFs to meet modern standards and help improve page load speed once implemented.

Concise copy

Although the top fold copy was out of scope for now, we could still improve the rest of the copy to be easier to digest. One idea was to present key information in short bullet points to accompany the new product visuals, rather than lengthy paragraphs. It is worth noting that the existing copy had been carefully crafted by the marketing team to optimise SEO. Therefore, it is important that design and marketing work together to find the best solution.

Final tests

To ensure that my proposed solutions solved the problem, another usability test was conducted on a prototype of the new homepage/landing page. This time all participants demonstrated a good understanding of Disciple’s purpose and identified its key benefits.

“It's a tool to build a community. You can modify colours, publish things and do livestreams.”

A/B test

It was decided that each improvement would be A/B tested separately before launch. While this would take longer than I desired, this would ensure that if any turned out to have a negative impact, they would not affect the results of other improvements.

Launch in progress

As of my departure from Disciple, my first improvement has been fully implemented, increasing the conversion rate by 24%. I look forward to seeing the others go live and am confident they will have similar results.
